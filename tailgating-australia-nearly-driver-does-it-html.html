Tailgating in Australia: Nearly EVERY driver does it

It is one of the most hated things we experience on the roads - and yet almost all of us tailgate others when we are behind the wheel. 

Research reveals tailgating is the most frequent form of road rage, the most irritating habit and the most common cause of crashes - but nearly all of us admit being guilty of doing it to other drivers.

An extraordinary 98 per cent of road users in a Queensland study by 887 licensed drivers admitted they had tailgated others at some point. 

Official advice is to leave three seconds between you and the car in front - which is the equivalent of 50 metres in a 60km/h zone. 

On the freeway it's between 84 and 92 metres, depending on whether it's a 100km/h or 110km/h limit.

Research and the everyday experience of almost all drivers says this advice is seldom followed, most likely because the chances of being caught are low.

The research, by the University of the Sunshine Coast, also found drivers who don't get caught are more likely to continue the behaviour and that drivers believe there is a low chance of being caught, the Conversation reported.

'In Queensland, Australia, over 7,000 injuries and fatalities were attributed to tailgating between 2019 and 2020. However, only 3,120 drivers received an infringement notice for the behaviour in this period.'

The findings followed research by Finder.com which said tailgating it is the most prevalent type of road rage experienced. 

Strangely a lower proportion of people said it had been done to them (49 per cent) than had admitted tailgating others in the university study.

Road users' perceptions of the consequences of tailgating don't match reality though. 

A survey by Budget Direct found drivers believed tailgating was the third most common cause of accidents.

But data from NSW traffic accidents between 2016 and 2020 actually showed rear end accidents were by far the most common crash type in metropolitan areas in the state.

Previously ING found Australian drivers voted tailgating the most irritating habit by others drivers. 

A Melbourne driving school instructor claimed aggressive driving isn't always to blame.

'They’re thinking about what they’re doing during the day and a lot of times I find that people don’t even realise they’re tailgating,' Mitch Batson, owner of MB Driving School, told 3AW.

Often its because drivers are distracted by thinking about work or even using digital devices, such as touching phones or being on calls, he said.

Mr Batson said 'two to three' seconds between cars was advisable, and even more in bad weather.

'Especially with Melbourne, where we get four seasons in one day, but there's no change in driving habits [in bad weather],' he said.

3AW host Dee Dee Dunleavy admitted on air that she sometimes drove up behind other cars 'just so they get the message, pull across'. 

Regardless of the reason for tailgating, it incurs heavy fines and a loss of demerit points in all states and territories.

In NSW tailgating penalties are severe: three demerit points and a $448 fine. 

In Victoria being caught for following too closely will see one demerit point taken from your licence.

In Queensland it's also one point off your licence and a $321 fine. 

Then there's the matter of who pays the insurance bill in the event of a crash. 

'If you run into the person in front its going to be [your] fault,' Mr Batson said.
