Comforting Self-Care Tips For Transitioning Into Autumn

As the days grow shorter and the nights draw in, we can often find ourselves struggling to adjust – especially given the contrast with summer months spent outdoors. However, autumn is a wonderful opportunity to rest and reflect, slow down, and lean into a cosy change in routine.

If you find the darker months difficult, it's important to take extra care of yourself – both physically and mentally – to make the season as manageable (and enjoyable) as possible. Keep reading to discover our top self-care tips for autumn.

1. Try a gratitude list dedicated to autumn

From grey skies to staying indoors, when it comes to a distinct decline in weather, a dip in mood is almost inevitable.

To bring back some positivity to your mindset, Lili Sinclair-Williams, the founder and CEO of LSW London, suggests starting your day with a gratitude practice: "As the days become shorter and the weather gets colder, focus on three things you are grateful for each morning.

"Focus on the joys that autumn brings, the special moments with your family around the fireplace, the excuse to stay in and get out the board games, the beauty of nature as the leaves turn a deep auburn. Practising gratitude each day will help your mindset to become more positive and help you to look out for the beauty and joys this new season brings.

"Create a 'happy list' (which you can add to at any time) of all the cosy things you can do in autumn that will bring you happiness. Perhaps it's lighting a scented candle, running a hot bubble bath, heading out for a brisk walk to breathe in the autumn air, drinking a hot mug of tea or getting snuggly in your chunky knitted sweaters. Try to implement at least one of these things into your daily routine so you can find moments of joy in the season and prioritise self-care."

Brent Darby Photography LTD

2. Give Sober October a go

You might be familiar with Dry January, but have you heard of Sober October? The movement encourages people to go alcohol-free in October to raise money for Macmillan Cancer Support. But, no matter the date you start, cutting down on alcohol, in general, can be a great way to improve your wellbeing – worthwhile if your mood dips in autumn or winter.

James Higgins, sleep specialist and CEO of Ethical Bedding, shares the positives of quitting alcohol: "A good night's kip is a critical factor for mental wellbeing as it aids in processing emotions, managing stress, and maintaining emotional balance.

"Alcohol can upset the balance of chemicals in the brain that send messages between nerve cells. It can make some chemicals less active, leading to tiredness and low mood, while making others more active, causing calmness. This imbalance can lead to feelings of depression.

"Additionally, sobriety enhances cognitive function, which is your ability to think, learn, remember, and solve problems. Quitting the booze can support improved decision-making, memory, and attention spans, contributing to overall better mental health."

Brent Darby Photography LTD

3. Layer up and get outside

There are countless benefits of being outdoors, and although the weather may be less than inviting, as the old saying goes, there is no such thing as bad weather, only bad clothes! Layering up and heading out (remember, there's always a hot shower when you return), works wonders both physically and mentally.

Dr Suzanne Bartlett Hackenmiller, Chief Medical Advisor at AllTrails, explains: "As we head into conditions that are slightly colder and darker, the effects of immersing yourself in nature can be even more profound as people are often feeling a bit depleted during this time of year.

"Try an easy night walk with a friend, partner or family member. With increased darkness, exposing yourself to the dark via a nighttime walk naturally releases melatonin to help you get a better night of sleep. Better sleep means a better routine and a healthier lifestyle!

"Studies have shown that walking in a calm and peaceful environment can boost the production of feel-good chemicals such as endorphins, improving overall mood - so why not layer up and enjoy the autumn colours? Slow meditative walking, in particular, can be especially beneficial by improving mindfulness and ultimately leading to a greater sense of wellbeing.

"Making the small, conscious effort to reconnect with the outside world can make all the difference in how one feels, and time in nature has been widely demonstrated to have significant benefits on both our physical and mental health, from boosting self-esteem to aiding anxiety and depression. Other studies have demonstrated that walking outdoors, instead of indoors on a treadmill, can have an even greater effect on boosting creativity, facilitating the free flow of ideas."

Brent Darby Photography LTD

4. Use light to wake you up

A good night's sleep is key to a restful autumn, but it can be hard to adjust to the change in daylight hours and an increased amount of time spent in darkness.

Speaking at Sleepeezee’s recent Zzzen event, sleep expert for Mattress Online, James Wilson, revealed the importance of light when it comes to getting us out of bed in the morning. "As the mornings get darker, it makes it more difficult to skip out of bed. A sunshine or daylight alarm clock pulls you out of sleep more gently than an audible alarm, meaning you don't wake with such a start and you don't fall into snooze button syndrome, pressing snooze over and over again.

He also stresses the importance of temperature in your bedroom: "As the season changes from summer to autumn, the change in temperature can sometimes make it harder to sleep.

"At this time of year, we might put the heating on and often make the bedroom too hot, making it harder to get to sleep, and making it more likely that we will wake in the night all hot and sweaty. Remember, the room should be slightly cooler than the rest of the house, so maybe turn the thermostat on your radiator down in the bedroom."

Nato Welton/Country Living

5. Upgrade your bed for hibernation season

Snuggling up is one of the best things about autumn, and as previously noted, time spent recharging in bed is key to feeling rested. To feel truly toasty, it's important to have plenty of cosy essentials on hand, and this includes not only your duvet but your mattress too.

"With autumn comes a significant drop in temperature and darker days which can make it much more difficult for us to get to sleep," say the experts at Sleepeezee. "One of the most important things to consider when finding the most suitable bedding for each season is your mattress fillings and duvets tog rating. In terms of mattresses, we would recommend a mattress with natural fillings such as wool which are brilliant for regulating body temperature to keep cool in summer and warmer in winter.
